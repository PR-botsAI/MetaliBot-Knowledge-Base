# 🔬 Metabolic Testing Guide — Know Your Numbers

> The most important lab tests for metabolic health — what to order, what they mean, optimal ranges (not just "normal" ranges), and what to do with the results. Knowing your numbers transforms you from passive patient to informed health architect.

---

## The Problem With "Normal" Ranges

Medical "normal" ranges are statistical — they represent the middle 95% of the population. In a population where 70%+ is overweight and 50% is pre-diabetic, "normal" doesn't mean optimal.

**Two different standards:**
- **Lab normal:** You won't die immediately and your doctor won't prescribe medication
- **Functional optimal:** Your body is running at genuine biological peak performance

This guide uses **functional optimal ranges** throughout.

## TIER 1: Essential Metabolic Panel

*These are the non-negotiables. Order these first.*

### Fasting Glucose
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | 70–85 mg/dL |
| Acceptable | 85–99 mg/dL |
| Prediabetes | 100–125 mg/dL |
| Diabetes | 126+ mg/dL |

**Clinical insight:** A fasting glucose above 90 mg/dL already indicates early insulin resistance. Many physicians ignore this until 126 mg/dL. Don't wait.

### A1c (Glycosylated Hemoglobin)
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | Below 5.0% |
| Good | 5.0–5.6% |
| Prediabetes | 5.7–6.4% |
| Diabetes | 6.5%+ |
| Target for diabetics | Below 6.0% |

**How often:** Every 3 months if managing metabolic disease; every 6 months for monitoring.

### Fasting Insulin
**This is the single most important and most ignored test for metabolic health.**

Fasting glucose can look normal while insulin is wildly elevated (the pancreas compensates by pumping more insulin).

| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | 2–5 μIU/mL |
| Acceptable | 5–10 μIU/mL |
| Insulin resistance | 10–25 μIU/mL |
| Severe insulin resistance | 25+ μIU/mL |

**HOMA-IR (calculate yourself):**
Formula: (Fasting insulin × Fasting glucose) ÷ 405
- Below 1.0: Insulin sensitive (excellent)
- 1.0–1.9: Borderline
- 2.0–2.9: Early insulin resistance
- 3.0+: Significant insulin resistance

### Triglycerides
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | Below 100 mg/dL |
| Borderline | 100–149 mg/dL |
| High | 150–199 mg/dL |
| Very high | 200–499 mg/dL |
| Dangerous | 500+ mg/dL |

**Clinical insight:** Triglycerides are the most direct marker of refined carbohydrate intake. They drop dramatically within 2–3 weeks of the 3×1 diet. A triglyceride level is essentially a report card on carbohydrate intake.

### HDL Cholesterol
| Status | Men | Women |
|--------|-----|-------|
| Optimal | Above 60 mg/dL | Above 70 mg/dL |
| Acceptable | 45–60 mg/dL | 50–70 mg/dL |
| Low (risk) | Below 40 mg/dL | Below 50 mg/dL |

**Triglyceride:HDL ratio — the best cardiovascular risk marker:**
- Below 2.0: Excellent insulin sensitivity
- 2.0–3.5: Moderate risk
- Above 3.5: Significant insulin resistance
- Above 6.0: Severe metabolic dysfunction

### LDL Cholesterol
**Context is everything.** Standard LDL alone is a poor predictor. What matters:
- LDL particle size (ask for NMR LipoProfile)
- Pattern A (large, fluffy) = low risk even if LDL is elevated
- Pattern B (small, dense) = high risk even if LDL looks normal

**Standard ranges:** Below 100 mg/dL (conventional); optimal is debated in metabolic medicine.

## TIER 2: Inflammation & Liver

### hsCRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | Below 0.5 mg/L |
| Low risk | 0.5–1.0 mg/L |
| Moderate | 1.0–3.0 mg/L |
| High risk | Above 3.0 mg/L |

**What raises CRP:** Refined carbohydrates, Candida toxins, visceral fat, gut dysbiosis, omega-6 excess, poor sleep, chronic stress.

**What lowers CRP:** 3×1 diet, omega-3 fatty acids, Candida cleanse, magnesium, turmeric/curcumin, weight loss.

### Liver Enzymes (AST, ALT, GGT)
| Test | Optimal | Caution |
|------|---------|----------|
| AST | Below 25 U/L | Above 40 |
| ALT | Below 25 U/L | Above 40 |
| GGT | Below 25 U/L | Above 30 |

**GGT is the most sensitive early liver stress marker** — rises before AST/ALT in fatty liver.

**Clinical note:** Elevated liver enzymes + high triglycerides + central obesity = fatty liver diagnosis (even without imaging). The 3×1 diet alone typically normalizes these within 8–12 weeks.

### Ferritin
| Status | Men | Women (premenopausal) |
|--------|-----|----------------------|
| Optimal | 50–100 ng/mL | 30–70 ng/mL |
| Low (iron deficiency) | Below 30 | Below 15 |
| High (iron overload / inflammation) | Above 150 | Above 100 |

**Dual role:** Ferritin is both an iron storage marker AND an acute phase reactant (rises with inflammation). Very high ferritin can mean iron overload OR severe inflammation.

## TIER 3: Thyroid Panel

*Order the FULL panel — not just TSH.*

| Test | Optimal Range | Units |
|------|--------------|-------|
| TSH | 0.5–2.0 | mIU/L |
| Free T4 | 1.1–1.8 | ng/dL |
| Free T3 | 3.2–4.2 | pg/mL |
| Reverse T3 (rT3) | Below 15 | ng/dL |
| Free T3:rT3 ratio | Above 20 | — |
| TPO antibodies | Below 35 | IU/mL |
| TG antibodies | Below 20 | IU/mL |

**Critical:** TSH alone is not sufficient. A person can have TSH of 2.0 ("normal") but Free T3 of 2.8 ("low") due to conversion failure at the liver. They will have hypothyroid symptoms that their doctor dismisses because TSH is "normal."

## TIER 4: Hormones

### Vitamin D (25-OH Vitamin D)
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Deficient | Below 20 ng/mL |
| Insufficient | 20–29 ng/mL |
| Functional minimum | 30–39 ng/mL |
| Optimal | 50–80 ng/mL |
| Therapeutic (physician supervised) | 80–100 ng/mL |
| Toxicity concern | Above 150 ng/mL |

### Testosterone (Men)
| Status | Total T | Free T |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Optimal (functional medicine) | 700–1,100 ng/dL | 15–25 pg/mL |
| Low-normal (conventional "acceptable") | 300–700 ng/dL | 8–15 pg/mL |
| Low (hypogonadism) | Below 300 ng/dL | Below 8 pg/mL |

**Clinical note:** Total testosterone can be "normal" while free testosterone is low due to high SHBG. Always test BOTH.

**SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin):** Optimal: 25–50 nmol/L (men). High SHBG binds testosterone, leaving little free.

### Cortisol (4-Point Salivary)
**Single blood cortisol is mostly useless.** The pattern throughout the day matters.

| Time | Optimal Salivary Cortisol |
|------|---------------------------|
| 7–8am (upon waking) | 13–24 nmol/L (highest of day) |
| Noon | 5–10 nmol/L |
| 4–5pm | 3–8 nmol/L |
| 10–11pm | Below 1.8 nmol/L |

**Abnormal patterns:**
- High throughout: Chronic stress, early HPA axis dysfunction
- Flat (low throughout): HPA axis exhaustion ("adrenal fatigue")
- Inverted (low morning, high evening): Circadian disruption, insomnia

## TIER 5: Minerals & Micronutrients

### Magnesium (RBC Magnesium)
**Serum magnesium is misleading** — the body maintains serum at the expense of intracellular levels.

| Status | RBC Magnesium |
|--------|---------------|
| Optimal | 5.5–6.5 mg/dL |
| Deficient | Below 4.5 mg/dL |

**Clinical note:** Most people with "normal" serum magnesium are still functionally deficient in intracellular magnesium.

### Zinc (Plasma Zinc)
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | 90–110 μg/dL |
| Deficient | Below 70 μg/dL |

### Selenium (Serum)
| Status | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Optimal | 120–180 μg/L |
| Deficient | Below 80 μg/L |
| Toxicity concern | Above 400 μg/L |

## TIER 6: Advanced Testing

### Organic Acids Test (OAT)
Provides comprehensive metabolic snapshot from urine:
- Mitochondrial function markers
- Neurotransmitter metabolism
- Vitamin deficiency markers
- Candida and fungal markers (arabinitol)
- Bacterial dysbiosis markers
- Detoxification capacity

**Best for:** People with complex symptoms not responding to standard interventions. The OAT tells you WHERE in the metabolic chain the problem lies.

### HTMA (Hair Tissue Mineral Analysis)
- 90-day mineral status (not just what's in blood right now)
- Heavy metal burden
- Mineral ratios (calcium:magnesium, zinc:copper, sodium:potassium)
- Identifies patterns of adrenal stress, thyroid patterns, metabolic type

### Advanced Lipid Panel (NMR LipoProfile)
- LDL particle number (LDL-P) — better predictor than LDL-C
- LDL particle size (Pattern A vs. B)
- HDL particle number and size
- Small dense LDL (the inflammatory kind)

### Comprehensive Stool Analysis
- Microbiome species composition and diversity
- Candida levels (objective quantification)
- Pathogenic bacteria
- Intestinal permeability markers (zonulin, calprotectin)
- Digestive enzyme function (elastase, fat malabsorption)
- Inflammatory markers (calprotectin, lactoferrin)

## HOME MONITORING TOOLS

### Glucometer (Most Important)
**Every metabolically-minded person should own one.**

**Test schedule:**
- Morning fasting glucose (first thing upon waking)
- 1 hour after any meal you want to test
- Use the 12-point rule: Rise >12 points = that food is an aggressor for YOUR body

**Target readings:**
- Fasting: Below 85 mg/dL (optimal)
- 1-hour postprandial: Below 140 mg/dL (optimal below 120 mg/dL)
- 2-hour postprandial: Below 120 mg/dL

### Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM)
Devices like Dexterity, Libre, or Levels provide real-time glucose data:
- See exactly which foods spike YOUR glucose
- See how stress, sleep, and exercise affect glucose
- Make dietary adjustments based on real data, not general guidelines
- The most powerful personalization tool available without a doctor

### Blood Pressure Monitor
**Target:** Below 120/80 mmHg. Optimal: 110–115/70–75.
Monitor in the morning before coffee. High BP often the first sign of insulin resistance and magnesium/potassium deficiency.

### Basal Body Temperature (Thyroid Monitor)
Protocol: Armpit temperature before getting out of bed.
- Below 97.8°F: Possible hypothyroidism
- 97.8–98.2°F: Normal
- Track for 5+ consecutive days for reliable pattern

## THE MINIMUM ANNUAL METABOLIC PANEL

*If you only order one panel per year, make it this:*

1. Fasting glucose
2. Fasting insulin
3. A1c
4. Complete lipid panel (with triglycerides)
5. hsCRP
6. Full thyroid panel (TSH, Free T4, Free T3, TPO antibodies)
7. 25-OH Vitamin D
8. Complete metabolic panel (kidney, liver function)
9. Ferritin
10. DHEA-S (adrenal health marker)

**Calculate yourself:**
- HOMA-IR = (insulin × glucose) ÷ 405
- Triglyceride:HDL ratio = triglycerides ÷ HDL
- T3:rT3 ratio = Free T3 (in pg/mL) ÷ rT3 (in ng/dL) × 100
