## What Is Metabolism — The Simple Definition

> *"Metabolism: the sum of all the movements, actions and changes that occur in the body to convert food and nutrients into energy for survival."*

All body processes involve movement: digestion, absorption, respiration, immune system, circulation, elimination. Movement requires energy. When the metabolism is **slow**, all these processes run slowly — this manifests as constipation, toxin accumulation, poor circulation, frequent infections, poor digestion, obesity.

**"Slow metabolism"** simply means the body's processes are not running at optimal speed.

## The Enemies of Metabolism (Los Enemigos del Metabolismo)

From the book's chapter structure, the metabolic enemies are:

1. **Excess sugars and refined carbohydrates** — #1 enemy. Creates glucose spikes, insulin floods, fat storage, Candida overgrowth.

2. **Dehydration** — Most people are chronically dehydrated. Formula: weight in lbs ÷ 2 = oz of water needed daily.

3. **Thyroid gland problems** — Hypothyroidism (often subclinical, missed by standard tests). Body temperature test: measure armpit temperature in the morning before getting up. Normal: 97.8°F–98.2°F. Below 97.8°F = possible hypothyroidism.

4. **Stress and cortisol** — Cortisol is a fat-storage hormone. Chronic stress = chronic fat accumulation, especially abdominal. Stress is physiologically documented to cause weight gain.

5. **Poor digestion** — Undigested food cannot nourish cells. Creates toxins, inflammation, poor nutrient absorption.

6. **Enemy substances** — Certain artificial sweeteners, preservatives, food dyes, industrial oils.

7. **Food intolerances** — Individual reactions to specific foods that create inflammation and slow metabolism. TAM (Wheat, Rice, Corn) are most common.

8. **Poor breakfast** — Skipping breakfast or eating a sugary breakfast starts the day with an insulin spike that sets the metabolic tone for the entire day.

9. **Candida Albicans — "The Silent Epidemic"** — A full chapter dedicated to this. Frank called it a "silent epidemic" because it affects the vast majority of overweight people and almost no one knows it.

10. **Hormonal issues in women** — A specific chapter addresses how female hormones affect metabolism.

11. **Antidepressants and metabolism-slowing medications** — A documented chapter on drug-induced metabolic damage.

12. **Sedentary lifestyle** — Lack of movement directly slows all metabolic processes.

## Losing Weight vs. Getting Slim — A Critical Distinction

This is one of the most important concepts in the metabolic approach:

**"Bajar de peso" (Losing weight)** = You can lose weight by losing muscle, water, or bone mass. This is what crash diets achieve. The scale goes down but you get FATTER in the long run because you destroyed the tissue that burns fat.

**"Adelgazar" (Getting slim/burning fat)** = Losing specifically FAT while maintaining or building muscle. This requires:
- Adequate protein intake (preserves muscle)
- Moderate carbohydrate reduction (reduces insulin, allows fat burning)
- Proper hydration (enables fat mobilization)
- Adequate minerals (magnesium + potassium)

**The goal is ALWAYS to adelgazar (get slim) — not just bajar de peso (lose weight).** This distinction is what separates the metabolic approach from conventional dieting.

## Carbohydrate Addiction — It's Physiologically Real

Carbohidrates are physiologically addictive. The mechanism:

1. Sugar and refined carbohydrates trigger beta-endorphin release in the brain
2. Beta-endorphins are natural morphine-like chemicals that produce pleasure and reduce pain
3. The body becomes dependent on this beta-endorphin release
4. Cravings for bread, sweets, sodas = a withdrawal state, not a lack of willpower

**This is why "just eat less" doesn't work.** You're asking someone to overcome a physiological addiction with willpower alone. The solution is to change the diet composition, address Candida (which also drives carb cravings), and ensure adequate minerals — not white-knuckle willpower.

## The Snacks (Meriendas) Strategy

Proper snacking between meals is important for:
- Preventing blood glucose drops (hypoglycemia between meals)
- Preventing the body from entering starvation mode
- Maintaining consistent energy levels

**Good snack choices (all Type A / Type S):**
- Nuts (almonds, walnuts, pecans)
- Cheese (any natural cheese)
- Eggs (boiled, deviled)
- Avocado
- Raw vegetables with cheese
- A small amount of protein

**Avoid:** Crackers, chips, fruit juice, granola bars, "healthy" commercial snack bars (usually loaded with sugar).

3. **Hypothyroidism** — Often subclinical, missed by tests. Check morning body temperature.
4. **Fatty liver** — Cannot process fat. Needs cleansing.
5. **Dehydration** — Fat cannot be mobilized without adequate water.
6. **Magnesium/potassium deficiency** — Cells cannot use glucose without these minerals.
7. **Stress** — High cortisol actively prevents fat release from cells.
8. **Poor sleep** — Lack of sleep raises cortisol and disrupts metabolic hormones.

## Intelligent Cheating (Cómo Hacer Trampa de Forma Inteligente)

An important practical concept: **perfection is not required, sustainability is.**

Rules for intelligent cheating:
- Never skip the 3×1 proportion at main meals
- Cheat with **quality** (dark chocolate vs. candy bar)
- Cheat in **small amounts** (a taste, not a plateful)
- Never use a "cheat" as punishment motivation ("I'll cheat today and be good tomorrow" → tomorrow never comes)
- Don't cheat with YOUR known aggressors — those are non-negotiable

**The goal is a lifestyle change that lasts forever** — not a temporary perfect diet that you eventually abandon.
